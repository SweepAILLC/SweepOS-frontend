# Pitching SOP
**Category:** Sales SOP | **Powers:** Call Library — audits pitch quality on every synced call

---

## Purpose

This SOP defines the standard for evaluating the **pitch portion** of a sales call — the moment the salesperson transitions from understanding the prospect's story to presenting the offer.

A strong pitch does not introduce a new conversation. It **continues** the discovery conversation by positioning deliverables as the natural, logical next step toward goals the prospect already named. The prospect should feel: *"This was built for me — not sold to me."*

**Prerequisite:** Discovery must have surfaced root pain, daily-life impact, tangible goals, and intangible goals. Pitching without that foundation is generic persuasion and scores poorly on this SOP.

---

## The Pitching Standard

The pitch has one job: **make the offer feel like the inevitable bridge between where they are and where they said they want to be.**

That requires five things:
1. **Pain weaving** — specific pains from discovery are named back and carried into the pitch.
2. **Natural solution framing** — deliverables are positioned as the logical next step, not a product dump.
3. **Goal bridge** — tangible and intangible goals from discovery are explicitly connected to outcomes the offer produces.
4. **Positioning clarity** — the mechanism, category, and "why this approach" are clear without jargon overload.
5. **Credibility in context** — proof is woven in at the right moment without derailing the narrative.

---

## Dimension 1: Pain Weaving

**What it measures:** Did the salesperson carry the prospect's **specific pains** from discovery into the pitch — or did they pitch a generic offer?

**The standard:**
- Pains named in discovery are **quoted or paraphrased** in the pitch ("You said you've been stuck at X for Y months because…").
- The pitch opens or anchors on **their** problem language, not the salesperson's feature list.
- Root pain (not surface complaint) is referenced — the emotional layer, not just "you want more clients."

**Failure signals:**
- Pitch starts with credentials, company history, or package overview before referencing their situation.
- Generic pain language ("a lot of people struggle with…") instead of their specific story.
- No callback to anything said in discovery — pitch could have been given to anyone.

**Scoring:**

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10 | Multiple specific pains woven in; prospect's own words echoed; root pain referenced |
| 7–8 | At least one specific pain referenced with some depth |
| 5–6 | Vague reference to "your situation" without specifics |
| 3–4 | Generic pain category only ("people in your position…") |
| 1–2 | No pain weaving — pure product pitch |

---

## Dimension 2: Natural Solution Framing

**What it measures:** Were deliverables positioned as the **natural next step** — or did the pitch feel like a hard pivot into selling?

**The standard:**
- Transition language bridges discovery → offer: "Based on everything you've shared, here's what I'd recommend…"
- Deliverables are framed as **solutions to named problems**, not a list of features.
- The prospect can draw a straight line: pain → mechanism → outcome.
- No "let me tell you about our program" energy — it's "here's how we solve exactly what you described."

**The Natural Solution Test:** If you removed the prospect's name and pains from the pitch, would it still make sense? If yes, it fails — the pitch wasn't personalized.

**Failure signals:**
- Abrupt transition ("Anyway, let me walk you through what we offer").
- Feature-dumping before establishing relevance.
- Prospect confusion about why this specific offer fits their situation.

**Scoring:**

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10 | Seamless bridge; deliverables feel inevitable; prospect affirms fit in real time |
| 7–8 | Clear connection between pains and offer; minor pivot feel |
| 5–6 | Offer presented but connection to their story is thin |
| 3–4 | Feature list with weak relevance framing |
| 1–2 | Hard sell with no bridge from discovery |

---

## Dimension 3: Goal Bridge

**What it measures:** Did the salesperson connect the offer to **both tangible and intangible goals** surfaced in discovery?

**The standard:**
- Tangible goals (numbers, timeframes) are tied to specific deliverables or outcomes: "The 90-day sprint is built to get you to $10k/month — the number you named."
- Intangible goals (identity, family, confidence) are acknowledged: "This isn't just about the revenue — it's about finally feeling like the person who follows through."
- The pitch answers: "How does this get me to [their goal]?" before the prospect has to ask.

**Failure signals:**
- Only logical/ROI framing with no emotional goal connection.
- Goals from discovery never referenced in the pitch.
- Salesperson assumes goals without confirming they match what was said earlier.

**Scoring:**

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10 | Tangible + intangible goals both explicitly bridged to offer outcomes |
| 7–8 | At least one goal type bridged with specificity |
| 5–6 | Goals mentioned but not tied to deliverables |
| 3–4 | Generic outcome promises ("you'll grow," "you'll feel better") |
| 1–2 | No goal connection |

---

## Dimension 4: Positioning Clarity

**What it measures:** Is it clear **what** the offer is, **why** this approach vs alternatives, and **how** it works at a high level?

**The standard:**
- The prospect understands the category/mechanism (not just the price).
- Differentiation is clear — why this vs what they've tried or what competitors offer.
- Complexity is appropriate: enough detail to believe, not so much they tune out.
- Named enemy or wrong-cause framing when relevant ("The problem isn't more content — it's the system behind it").

**Failure signals:**
- Prospect asks "wait, so what exactly do I get?" after the pitch.
- Jargon-heavy explanation with no plain-language summary.
- No differentiation — sounds like every other offer in the space.

**Scoring:**

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10 | Crystal clear mechanism + differentiation; prospect summarizes it back accurately |
| 7–8 | Mostly clear; minor confusion resolved quickly |
| 5–6 | Understandable but generic or muddled on differentiation |
| 3–4 | Confusing; prospect needs multiple clarifications |
| 1–2 | Unclear what they're buying or why this approach |

---

## Dimension 5: Credibility in Context

**What it measures:** Was proof/credibility woven in **at the right moment** to support the pitch — without derailing the narrative?

**The standard:**
- Proof points are **relevant** to the prospect's situation (similar client, similar pain, similar goal).
- Credibility supports the mechanism claim, not ego ("I've been doing this 10 years" without tying to their outcome).
- Social proof is peer-level when possible ("someone in your exact position…").
- Proof doesn't replace pain weaving — it reinforces it.

**Failure signals:**
- Credential-dumping at the start of the pitch.
- Irrelevant case studies that don't match prospect's pain/goal.
- No proof when prospect signals skepticism.

**Scoring:**

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10 | Timely, relevant proof that increases belief without breaking flow |
| 7–8 | Good proof used; slightly mistimed or generic |
| 5–6 | Some proof but weak relevance or too much/too little |
| 3–4 | Credential listing without contextual proof |
| 1–2 | No credibility or proof that hurts trust |

---

## Pitch Score Calculation

```
Pitch Score (0–100) =
  (Pain Weaving × 20)
  + (Natural Solution Framing × 20)
  + (Goal Bridge × 20)
  + (Positioning Clarity × 20)
  + (Credibility in Context × 20)
```

Each dimension scored 1–10, then multiplied by 20.

**Interpretation:**

| Score | Verdict |
|-------|---------|
| 90–100 | Elite pitch — prospect feels the offer was built for them |
| 75–89 | Strong pitch — minor gaps won't kill the close |
| 60–74 | Adequate — works for warm buyers, loses skeptical ones |
| 40–59 | Weak — feels like a generic sales presentation |
| Below 40 | Pitch disconnected from discovery — close requires luck |

---

## Pitch Structure Checklist

Before transitioning to price/close:

- [ ] At least one specific pain from discovery named in the pitch
- [ ] Deliverables framed as solutions to those pains (not a feature list)
- [ ] Tangible goal from discovery connected to an outcome
- [ ] Intangible goal acknowledged (identity, family, confidence, etc.)
- [ ] Mechanism/category clear in plain language
- [ ] Relevant proof point tied to their situation
- [ ] Prospect affirms fit or asks clarifying questions (buying signal)

---

## What Strong Pitching Sets Up

A pitch that scores 80+ means the prospect should understand:
- Why **this** offer fits **their** specific situation
- How it gets them to the goals **they** named
- Why **this approach** vs what they've tried
- That the salesperson **gets them** — the pitch felt like a mirror, not a brochure

From here, objections are easier to handle because the prospect isn't objecting to a stranger's pitch — they're negotiating with someone who already demonstrated understanding.
