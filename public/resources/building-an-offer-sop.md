# Building an Offer
**Category:** Business SOP | A system for creating an offer your market can't ignore — and can't replicate

---

## Preface

Your offer is not your product. It's the promise and the vehicle that closes the gap between where your client is and where they want to be.

Two forces determine whether an offer converts:

1. **Value** — Does the transformation feel real, fast, and low-effort?
2. **Positioning** — Does this offer feel different from the 50 other coaches selling something similar?

Most SOPs on offers only address #1. In a saturated market, #2 is what actually determines whether anyone stops scrolling to read #1.

---

## 1. Market Positioning (Do This Before Anything Else)

**Reasoning:** In coaching, the product (accountability + programming + check-ins) is nearly identical across competitors. Prospects can't evaluate quality before buying, so they default to comparing price or trusting whoever seems most different. Undifferentiated offers compete on price. Differentiated offers compete on relevance.

### Step 1: Identify the Category You're Actually In

Prospects mentally file you into a category before they read your offer. If you're in the same category as everyone else ("online fitness coach"), you're compared on price by default.

**Fill in:**
- Default category prospects would put me in: _______________
- What that category is known for (good and bad): _______________

**Action:** Either dominate a sub-category no one owns yet, or create one. Example: not "online coach" but "the coach for dads who used to be athletes." The narrower category has less competition and higher relevance.

---

### Step 2: Name the Enemy

**Reasoning:** People don't buy a solution in a vacuum — they buy relief from something they've identified as the problem. Naming a specific enemy (not a competitor, a *cause*) gives your offer a reason to exist beyond "I also do coaching."

**Fill in:**
- What my ICP wrongly blames for their lack of results: _______________
  (Examples: "willpower," "generic templates," "not enough cardio," "bad genetics")
- What I actually believe is the real cause: _______________

**Action:** Your marketing and offer should position against the wrong cause, not against other coaches. Example: "This isn't a discipline problem. It's a structure problem." This is differentiation without ever mentioning a competitor.

---

### Step 3: Own a Mechanism

**Reasoning:** "12 weeks of coaching" is a commodity. A named, proprietary-feeling method is not directly comparable to anyone else's offer, even if the underlying principles are similar. This is the single highest-leverage differentiation move available to a solo coach.

**Fill in:**
- My unique approach or sequence, named: _______________
  (Example: "The Rebuild Protocol," "Metabolic Reset Method")
- The 3 steps or pillars that make it up: _______________

**Action:** Every offer, piece of content, and sales call should reference this named mechanism repeatedly. Repetition builds proprietary perception even when the method is a reasonable synthesis of known principles.

---

### Step 4: Find Your Proof Asymmetry

**Reasoning:** In a saturated market, the coach with the most specific, most repeated proof wins — not the coach with the best program. Determine what you can prove that competitors can't or don't bother to.

**Fill in:**
- What client result or process can I document better than competitors typically do? _______________
  (Examples: before/after data at each 2-week mark, a public testimonial library, live coaching call recordings, longitudinal client retention)

**Action:** Build this proof asset once, reuse it everywhere. It becomes a moat if maintained consistently — most coaches don't have the discipline to document consistently, so this compounds.

---

### Positioning Statement (Combine Steps 1–4)

> **"I'm not another _______________ (category). I'm the _______________ (owned category/mechanism) for _______________ (specific ICP) who are done blaming _______________ (named enemy) for _______________ (their stuck result)."**

---

## 2. Start With the Dream Outcome

Positioning tells them why you're different. This tells them why they should care.

**Formula:** [Desired End State] + [Specific Timeframe] + [Emotional Payoff]

**Example:** "Lose 20 lbs in 12 weeks and finally feel confident in your own skin."

**Action:** Pull this language directly from discovery call transcripts. Client language converts better than coach language because it mirrors how the prospect already thinks about the problem — no translation required on their end.

---

## 3. Price the Value, Not the Time

**The Value Equation:**

`VALUE = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort Required)`

**Reasoning:** Perceived value is not tied to hours delivered. It's tied to how believable, fast, and low-effort the outcome feels. This is why identical deliverables priced and framed differently convert at different rates — you're not selling coaching, you're selling a probability shift.

**Weak framing:** "12-week program with workouts and meal plans."
**Strong framing:** "Lose 15–25 lbs in 90 days with done-for-you meal plans and daily accountability — without giving up the foods you like."

**Action:** Every offer description should make explicit claims about outcome, likelihood, timeframe, and effort. If any of the four is missing, the value is undersold regardless of what's actually included.

---

## 4. Build the Offer Stack

**Core Deliverable:** The primary mechanism (1-on-1 coaching, group program, hybrid).

**Supporting Components:** Increase perceived likelihood or reduce effort (custom programming, check-ins, tracking systems).

**Accelerators:** Make success feel automatic (done-for-you templates, quick-start guides, emergency protocols for when life gets disrupted).

**Action:** For each component, ask: "Does this increase likelihood of success, or decrease effort required?" If a component does neither, cut it. Bloated stacks dilute perceived value — they don't add to it.

---

## 5. Name the Offer to Reinforce Positioning

The name should encode your Step 3 mechanism, not just describe the outcome.

**Weak:** "12-Week Transformation Program" (describes outcome only, zero differentiation)
**Strong:** "The Rebuild Protocol" (encodes owned mechanism, unrepeatable by competitors without sounding like a copy)

**Action:** If a competitor could paste your offer name onto their own program with zero friction, the name isn't doing positioning work. Rename it.

---

## 6. Scarcity and Urgency (Use Honestly)

**Scarcity:** Caps on availability. "5 spots this quarter." Works because it's true, not because it's stated.

**Urgency:** Time-bound cost of waiting. "Price increases $500 on [date]," or consequence-based: "Every month without structure is a month further from the goal you named on our call."

**Reasoning:** Fabricated scarcity is detectable and destroys trust the first time a prospect notices "5 spots left" for the third consecutive month. Trust lost from one broken scarcity claim costs more in referral and retention value than any single sale gained from it.

**Action:** Only state a cap you will actually enforce. If you can't enforce it, use consequence-based urgency instead — it requires no capacity limit to be true.

---

## 7. Guarantee (Reverse the Risk)

**Outcome-based:** "Lose 15 lbs in 90 days or another 90 days free."
**Conditional:** "Not satisfied in 14 days, full refund."
**Stacked (strongest):** Combine both, plus a "worth 10x or I refund and pay you $500" clause.

**Reasoning:** The guarantee's job is to shift the prospect's risk calculation from "what if this doesn't work" to "what do I lose by trying." Stronger guarantees correlate with fewer refunds in practice, because clients who buy under a strong guarantee tend to be more convinced, not more skeptical — the guarantee itself is a conviction signal from you they absorb before starting.

**Action:** Write the strongest guarantee you can honor without resentment if invoked. If you'd feel bitter honoring it, it's too aggressive for your current confidence level in your delivery.

---

## 8. Stack and Show the Value

List each component with a standalone market value, sum it, then show the actual price against that sum.

**Example:**
- 12 weeks 1-on-1 coaching — $3,000
- Custom nutrition program — $500
- Weekly check-ins — $600
- 24/7 messaging support — $800
- **Total Value: $4,900 → Price: $1,997**

**Reasoning:** Anchoring the price against a larger, itemized total reframes the transaction from "cost" to "discount." This works only if each valuation is defensible — if a prospect could reasonably buy that component elsewhere for less, the anchor collapses and damages credibility on every other line item.

**Action:** Value each line item at what a prospect could realistically pay a specialist for that exact component alone. Don't inflate.

---

## 9. Choose a Pricing Model

| Model | Best For | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|
| One-time payment | High-ticket, short programs | Higher barrier, higher commitment |
| Payment plan | Accessibility | Refund/drop-off risk |
| Subscription | Ongoing coaching | Requires continuous value delivery |
| Hybrid (upfront + recurring) | Transformation + maintenance | More complex to explain |

**Anchor pricing:** Present highest tier first (Platinum $9,997 → Gold $4,997 → Silver $2,997). The middle tier absorbs most volume and feels reasonable by comparison.

**Action:** Charge a premium for payment plans relative to one-time price (e.g., $2,997 one-time vs. $3,297 split) to incentivize full payment without penalizing flexibility.

---

## 10. Bonuses (Only If They Compound the Offer)

Good bonus categories: quick-start assets (reduce time delay), risk-reversal tools (plateau protocols, travel guides), exclusivity (lifetime updates), and time-bound extras (book-by-Friday bonus call).

**Action:** Only add bonuses that are automated or reusable across clients. A bonus that creates new 1-on-1 labor per client is a hidden cost, not free value.

---

## 11. Present the Offer

Sequence: Restate the problem → introduce the named mechanism → explain how it works in 3 steps → stack the value → state price against the value anchor → scarcity/urgency → guarantee → direct close.

**Action:** Never skip the "restate the problem" step. Value stacks and guarantees only land if the prospect feels understood first — pitching value before pain amplification is the most common reason technically-good offers underperform on sales calls.

---

## 12. Test One Variable at a Time

Track: conversion rate (sales ÷ qualified leads), average order value, refund rate, and LTV.

**Test independently:** price, guarantee strength, positioning angle, bonus stack.

**Reasoning:** Changing two variables in the same test cycle makes the result unattributable. If price and guarantee both change and conversion moves, you don't know which lever to pull next.

**Action:** Run one change per 15–20 sales conversations minimum before concluding causality.

---

## 13. No-Brainer Checklist

- [ ] Positioning is distinct from category default
- [ ] Named enemy and mechanism are stated, not implied
- [ ] Dream outcome is specific, timed, and emotionally framed
- [ ] Value stack total clearly exceeds price
- [ ] Guarantee reverses risk without resentment if invoked
- [ ] Scarcity/urgency claims are 100% true
- [ ] Proof asset exists and is repeated across content and sales

If any box is unchecked, that's the next lever to pull — not a new offer entirely.

---

## The Core Principle

In a saturated market, the better product doesn't win by default. The more clearly positioned offer wins, because prospects can't evaluate quality pre-purchase but can immediately evaluate relevance and differentiation.

**Build the offer for value. Build the positioning for attention. You need both — value alone gets outcompeted on price, positioning alone gets outcompeted on trust.**
