# Discovery Call Audit SOP
**Category:** Sales SOP | **Powers:** Call Library — audits discovery quality on every synced call

---

## Purpose

This SOP defines the standard for evaluating the **discovery portion of a sales call only**. It does not cover the pitch, objection handling, or closing. Its sole purpose is to measure whether the salesperson built the foundation required for a credible, personalized pitch to work.

A pitch built on shallow discovery is a guess. A pitch built on this SOP's standard is a mirror — the prospect hears their own story reflected back at them with precision, and the close becomes a logical conclusion rather than a persuasion attempt.

---

## The Discovery Standard

Discovery has one job: **make the prospect feel completely understood before a single solution is mentioned.**

That requires four things to be present:
1. The real **pain** is identified and named — not a surface complaint, the root wound.
2. The pain is **grounded in daily life** — how it actually shows up, not just that it exists.
3. **Tangible goals** are made specific — measurable outcomes the prospect can picture.
4. **Intangible goals** are surfaced — the emotional and identity stakes underneath the numbers.

When all four are present, the salesperson holds proof that they understand this person's specific situation. That proof is the source of trust and authority — not credentials, not testimonials.

---

## Dimension 1: Pain Identification

**What it measures:** Did the salesperson find the real pain, or did they accept the surface complaint?

**The standard:**
- The prospect named a problem, and the salesperson probed past the first answer to find its root cause.
- The pain was **named explicitly** by the salesperson ("So what you're really experiencing is..."), not left vague.
- Follow-up questions were asked — one question is never enough. Surface complaints ("I want to lose weight," "I need more clients") always have a layer underneath them ("I've tried before and failed, so I've stopped trusting myself").

**Questions that reach the real pain:**
- "When did this first become a problem for you?"
- "What have you already tried, and why do you think it didn't work?"
- "What does this actually cost you — time, money, confidence?"
- "If I asked your [spouse/partner/team] what they'd say the real issue is, what would they say?"

**Failure signals:**
- Salesperson rephrased the prospect's first answer and moved on.
- No follow-up to an obvious surface complaint.
- Pain was only described at the category level ("wants to get fit," "wants to grow the business") with no specificity.

**Scoring:**

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10 | Root pain explicitly named, confirmed by prospect, specific and emotional |
| 7–8 | Real pain identified with some depth, not fully confirmed |
| 5–6 | Surface pain only, one layer of probing |
| 3–4 | Accepted the first answer, no probing |
| 1–2 | Pain not identified at all — moved to pitch without it |

## Dimension 2: Pain Impact — Daily Life Grounding

**What it measures:** Did the salesperson understand how the pain actually **shows up** in the prospect's day-to-day life?

**The standard:**
- The pain was connected to **specific daily behaviors, feelings, or situations** — not described in abstract.
- The salesperson asked how the pain manifests, not just what it is.
- The prospect described a real scene from their life — a specific morning, a specific moment, a specific relationship — and the salesperson acknowledged it.

**Questions that ground pain in daily life:**
- "Walk me through what a typical week looks like with this problem present."
- "What's the first thing you feel/think when you wake up and this problem is still there?"
- "How is this affecting the people around you — your family, your clients, your team?"
- "Give me a specific recent example — when did this show up in the last two weeks?"

**Why this matters:** Abstract pain creates weak urgency. A prospect who says "I want to feel better" is not close to buying. A prospect who says "I can't keep up with my kids on vacation and it's embarrassing — they look at me differently now" is close to buying. The difference is grounding.

**Failure signals:**
- Pain described at concept level only ("just general stress," "not where I want to be").
- No specific scenes, moments, or relationships named.
- Salesperson summarized without asking for specifics.

**Scoring:**

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10 | Specific scene/moment named, daily life impact emotional and concrete |
| 7–8 | Daily impact described with some specificity |
| 5–6 | General impact acknowledged, no specific scenes |
| 3–4 | Impact mentioned but not explored |
| 1–2 | Impact not addressed |

## Dimension 3: Tangible Goals — Quantified Outcomes

**What it measures:** Did the salesperson help the prospect define goals as **specific, measurable outcomes** — not wishes?

**The standard:**
- Every stated goal has a **number and a timeframe** attached by the end of discovery.
- The salesperson pushed past vague language ("I want to scale," "I want to lose weight") until the prospect named specific targets.
- Numbers came from the **prospect**, not suggested by the salesperson. Prospect-owned goals have far higher commitment.

**Examples of vague vs. quantified goals:**

| Vague (Unacceptable) | Quantified (Standard) |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| "I want to lose weight" | "20 lbs in 12 weeks" |
| "I want to make more money" | "$10,000/month within 90 days" |
| "I want to grow my business" | "3 new clients per month at $2,500 each" |
| "I want to get stronger" | "Bench 225 lbs and run a 5K before summer" |

**Questions that quantify tangible goals:**
- "If we worked together for [X months], what would success look like — give me a number."
- "What's the specific number that would make you feel like this was worth it?"
- "By what date do you want this solved?"
- "Why that number specifically — what changes when you hit it?"

**Failure signals:**
- Goals left at the wish level ("wants to feel better," "wants more freedom") without any specific metric.
- Salesperson suggested numbers instead of drawing them from the prospect.
- No timeframe attached to any goal.

**Scoring:**

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10 | 2+ goals fully quantified (number + timeframe), prospect-owned |
| 7–8 | At least 1 goal quantified, others still vague |
| 5–6 | Goals discussed but not pushed to specifics |
| 3–4 | Vague goals accepted without follow-up |
| 1–2 | Goals not identified |

---

## Dimension 4: Intangible Goals — Emotional and Identity Stakes

**What it measures:** Did the salesperson surface **why** the tangible goals matter — the identity, belonging, and emotional outcomes underneath the numbers?

**The standard:**
- Underneath every measurable goal is an emotional goal. A prospect who wants to make $10,000/month often wants to feel like they finally made it — to stop apologizing for their career, to stop watching others succeed while they stagnate, to prove something to themselves or someone they love.
- The salesperson asked "why" at least once after a goal was stated — not just what, but what it means.
- Intangible goals were **named explicitly** by the salesperson or confirmed by the prospect in emotional language.

**Categories of intangible goals:**

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Family / Relationships | "Make my spouse proud," "be present for my kids," "give my parents a better life" |
| Identity / Self-concept | "Finally feel like I deserve success," "stop being the person who starts and quits," "become who I said I'd be" |
| Legacy / Contribution | "Leave something behind," "give back to my community," "be someone others look up to" |
| Freedom / Autonomy | "Stop needing permission," "never miss a school event again," "control my own schedule" |
| Confidence / Self-trust | "Stop second-guessing everything," "walk into a room differently," "trust my own judgment" |

**Questions that surface intangible goals:**
- "Why does [the tangible goal] matter to you — what changes in your life when you hit that?"
- "Who else is affected when this stays unsolved?"
- "Who do you become when this is fixed?"
- "What would it mean about you if you didn't get this handled?"
- "If a year from now you've hit your goal — paint me the picture. What's different about your life?"

**Failure signals:**
- Conversation stayed entirely in metrics and tactics without touching the emotional layer.
- No "why" questions asked after goals were stated.
- Intangible goals present but not named or acknowledged — left as subtext instead of brought to the surface.

**Scoring:**

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10 | 2+ intangible goals named explicitly, emotional language from prospect, identity/legacy stakes present |
| 7–8 | At least 1 intangible goal named and acknowledged |
| 5–6 | Emotional undertone present but not explicitly named |
| 3–4 | Goals discussed only in logical/financial terms |
| 1–2 | No intangible goals surfaced |

## Dimension 5: Rapport, Trust, and Authority

**What it measures:** Did the salesperson create the conditions where the prospect **felt safe being honest** and **believed the salesperson understood them**?

**The standard:**
- Rapport is not small talk. It's the experience of being heard without judgment. It requires active listening signals, not just silence.
- Trust is built by demonstrating understanding, not by being likeable. When a salesperson accurately names what the prospect is feeling before the prospect says it, trust compounds fast.
- Authority in discovery means the salesperson's questions reveal expertise — not credential-listing. A question like "When you say you've been inconsistent, is that inconsistency with the habit itself or with the environment you're in?" signals mastery without stating it.

**Behaviors that build rapport and trust:**
- Paraphrasing and labeling emotions: "It sounds like it's not just the goal, it's that you've tried before and it didn't stick — and now you're not sure whether to trust yourself."
- Normalizing without dismissing: "Most people in your position feel that way. Here's what actually causes it."
- Patience — not rushing to fill silence after a vulnerable admission.
- Referring back to something said earlier: "You mentioned earlier that your family is watching this — does that feel like pressure or motivation?"

**Authority behaviors in discovery:**
- Asking questions prospects have never been asked before.
- Naming something the prospect hadn't articulated but immediately agrees with.
- Diagnosing the root cause before offering any solution.
- Saying "Before I can tell you whether I can help you, I need to understand a few things" — making the discovery feel like diagnosis, not a pitch setup.

**Failure signals:**
- Salesperson talked more than prospect listened.
- Questions were transactional, not curious.
- Vulnerable moments were passed over quickly.
- Authority came through credential mentions rather than through demonstrated understanding.

**Scoring:**

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10 | Prospect clearly felt heard; salesperson demonstrated mastery through questions; emotional safety evident |
| 7–8 | Rapport present, some trust built, authority demonstrated at least once |
| 5–6 | Comfortable exchange but surface-level; no notable trust-building moments |
| 3–4 | Transactional; prospect gave information but not vulnerable |
| 1–2 | Discovery felt like an interrogation or was skipped |

## Discovery Score Calculation

```
Discovery Score (0–100) =
  (Pain Identification × 20)
  + (Daily Life Grounding × 20)
  + (Tangible Goals × 20)
  + (Intangible Goals × 20)
  + (Rapport, Trust, Authority × 20)
```

Each dimension scored 1–10, then multiplied by 20.

**Interpretation:**

| Score | Verdict |
|-------|---------|
| 90–100 | Elite discovery — pitch will feel effortless because it mirrors the prospect's own words |
| 75–89 | Strong discovery — pitch has solid foundation; small gaps won't cost the deal |
| 60–74 | Adequate discovery — pitch will work for easy buyers, will lose skeptical ones |
| 40–59 | Weak discovery — pitch is likely generic; close rate will reflect it |
| Below 40 | Discovery was skipped — pitch is a cold presentation; close requires luck not skill |

## What Good Discovery Sets Up (But Does Not Include)

This SOP ends at the discovery phase. A complete discovery that scores 80+ means the salesperson should have in their notes:

- The specific root pain (in the prospect's exact words)
- The daily manifestation of that pain (a scene or recurring situation)
- At least one fully quantified tangible goal (number + timeframe, prospect-owned)
- At least one intangible goal (the emotional stakes)
- Evidence of genuine connection and trust

These four elements are the inputs to a strong pitch. Without them, even a perfect pitch falls flat. With them, the pitch is not a sales tactic — it's a proposal that already feels like it was written for this specific person.

---

## Quick-Reference Checklist

Before leaving discovery:

- [ ] Root pain identified and named (not just surface complaint)
- [ ] Salesperson can describe how pain shows up in prospect's daily life
- [ ] At least one tangible goal with a number and timeframe (prospect-supplied)
- [ ] At least one intangible goal named (identity, family, freedom, confidence, legacy)
- [ ] Prospect felt heard — there was at least one moment of emotional acknowledgment
- [ ] Salesperson demonstrated mastery through questions, not credentials
